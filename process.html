The Infinite Descent Deconstructing Digital Habituation

Unveiling and Visualizing the Architecture of Digital Addiction

01. Process
Deconstructing Digital Habituation

Research: How We Get Fooled

Why can't we put down our phones? Looking at my screen time—about 6 hours a day—I realized it's not just me lacking willpower. The apps are designed to trap us. I researched Brian Wansink’s "Bottomless Bowls" study, which shows how missing 'stopping cues' makes us keep scrolling without thinking. I also looked into Harry Brignull’s "Dark Patterns" to see how tricky UI takes away our control. Finally, James Williams's book helped me take the abstract idea of "wasted time" and turn it into a physical receipt that people have to face.

Initial Ideas

In the early stages, I had three main concepts. For outcome 1: The Attention Tax Receipt, Dark Patterns Dialog, Gaze Capture Poster (Tracking eye movement and visualize the action into a line). And for the outcome 2, I wanted to create a website that provide user to explore on either dark patterns or doomscrolling.

Work in Progress: Trials and Failures

Outcome 1 (The Receipt): At first, I tried to make a machine that endlessly rolled a 32-inch receipt that I created. However, it was very challenging to make such a machine that allows for continuous operation and action for scrolling. Also, it seems undetachable to users who explore the website and having not connected to the system. So, now I connected the printer to the web simulator, and it prints a personalized 'Time Tax' receipt based on the user's data.

Outcome 2 (The Web Trap): I coded a website for exploring dark patterns and doomscrolling and user can explore the different aspects of these actions and results base on thier experience. To make people doomscroll, I tested a few interactions. I made the buttons hard to see, added a subtle bouncing effect when the user stops scrolling to force them to move again, and made the Reels page automatically "like" a post if you double-tap.

Hardware: I used a Raspberry Pi and a touchscreen. Because the back components were exposed, I built a custom cover out of raw cardboard. I chose cardboard because its cheap, disposable look perfectly matched the vibe of the thermal receipt paper. However, the power cable kept disconnecting, so I had to use an iPad as a backup plan.

02. Final Outcomes

Outcome 1: The Attention Tax Receipt

This installation turns invisible digital waste into a physical waste. Connected to the web simulator, it uses a thermal printer to print a real-time "Time Tax Invoice" based on the user’s actions. It calculates things like scroll depth, dopamine spikes, and wasted time. The hardware is covered by a raw cardboard box to match the cheap, disposable feeling of the receipt paper. I included a random sentence by what user did on their survey. It forces users to physically hold the results of their bad digital habits and see the consequences.

Outcome 2: The Interactive Web-Trap

"Doomscroll Simulator" is a website designed to trap you in a doomscrolling loop. Using dark patterns, I made essential buttons like 'refresh' and 'exit' almost invisible. I also forced a 'like' action whenever a user double-taps the screen, and added bouncing animations to trigger unconscious scrolling. I kept the design super minimal, using "intentional emptiness" so there’s nothing else to focus on except the scrolling itself. It's a digital trap that tracks your behavior and screen time in real-time to show exactly how easily we lose control of our attention.

03. Reflection

Designer as Author

Working as a 'designer as author' let me direct the whole story of digital addiction. My biggest success was turning invisible web data into a real, physical system on a receipt. I’m really proud of how I made the cardboard cover match with the thermal paper—it made the "digital waste" concept much stronger.

However, I faced a huge challenge with the hardware. My biggest failure was the printing connection. When a user clicked the "Print Receipt" button on the screen, I wanted the receipt to come out immediately and magically. Instead, the system's default "Print Setup Page" popped up first. This extra step totally ruined the immersive trap I tried to build, which was my biggest challenge. Also, trying to critique digital waste by printing actual paper waste was a huge environmental irony that I need to figure out in the future. (but also I like the sequence of it)

Through Graphic Design 4, I learned I am strong at concepting and breaking down user behaviors, but I must improve my technical prototyping skills. My advice for my future self in the Graphic Design is test every idea much earlier, and never let minor problems and technical limits ruin the main concept. Last but not least, I learned that sheer volume of work does not guarantee success. Moving forward, I want to challenge my usual ways of thinking and actively embrace diverse perspectives to find smarter solutions.
